## Contemplation

A story...

The spring of 2020 was for me, as for many people, a very destabilizing time. The familiar normality of life had been replaced with... something else. Weekly lunches were cancelled. The university where I taught was completely empty. Stores were out of eggs and toilet paper for unknown reasons. Yet through this upheaval, a new normality emerged. And strangely it began to be pleasant. In the mornings I took to sitting on my porch to feel the sun on my face, drinking coffee and doing puzzles. Sodoku became a way for me to put order back in the world. But more than that, it was a time removed from the automatic engagement with the world. As the world emerged from lockdown over the next five years, that automatic reactive relationship has only intensified. We were only too happy to get back on the treadmill. (-nix cliche)

Contemplation can mean many things to many people. For some it is likened to meditation, a contemplative prayer that deepens ones relationship to the divine. %% [QUOTE] %% For some it is deep thinking, a focused consideration on some piece of art or aspect of nature. In their work on contemplative architecture, Djebbara et al [-@djebbaraContemplativeNeuroaestheticsArchitecture2024, 98] refer to it as a "heightened level of awareness and an intentional focus on the present moment, fostering a state of deep reflection and non-judgmental engagement." For some it is X. 

For my purposes, I consider the core aspect of contemplation a shifting of focus, an attunement to a different sort of something in the world. The word contemplation stems from the latin *com* + *templum*, and was originally "to mark out a space for observation" [@ContemplateEtymologyOrigin]. Through this understanding we can see that all manner of interactions fall under the umbrella of contemplation. %% [EXAMPLES] %%

The problem is...

In general, contemporary society has created a situation in which people feel overwhelmed, with fractured attention and the pressure to be continually active and productive. We act and act, without giving ourselves time to reflect, to contemplate the nature of the world. Philosopher Byung-Chul Han [-@hanVitaContemplativaPraise2023, 2] laments this loss of respite, saying "'leisure time' lacks both intensity of life and contemplation. It is a time that we kill so as not to get bored. It is not *free, living time*; it is *dead time*." In contrast, he claims "we owe true happiness to the useless and purposeless, to what is intentionally convoluted, what is unproductive, indirect, exuberant, superfluous, to beautiful forms and gestures that have no use and serve no purpose" [@hanVitaContemplativaPraise2023, 5]. He names this concept *ceremonious inactivity* where "we do something, but to no end," resulting in the discovery of a reality that "reveals itself only to contemplative attention" [@hanVitaContemplativaPraise2023, 11].

The _vita contemplativa_ stands in contrast to _vita activa_. The two are not in opposition, but work in concert to create the whole of experience. Yet, as Han argues, even our leisure time is active. "We are losing a sense for the kind of inactivity that is not an incapability, not a refusal, not just the absence of activity but a capacity in itself." Where we once had space to reflect now we only consume.

## Encouraging Contemplation

A few mechanisms to explore...

* Players (Attunement) - Designers can invite attunement. Through their agency (Nguyen) the players can enact a more attuned stance, shifting their focus from mastery/conquest to receptive, sustained, relational attention (Oliveros/Cardiff).

* Playthings (Enstrangement) - Designers can make the object/interface more 'enstranged' (Shlovsky). These objects resist automatic use. They resist flow. Perception is re-awakened through friction (Khaled).  

* Playgrounds (Allusivity) - Designers can create more 'allusive' (Seerveld) playspaces/magic circles (Huizinga, if we must). These spaces suggest rather than declare - with atmospheric cues, symbolic textures, and indirect meaning (as in contemplative Architecture).  

* Playtime (Ritualization) - Designers can ritualize time, focusing on depth rather than productive clock-time (Han). Relying on foreground/background-shifting (Eno) as well as repetition, returning, and ceremonious intervals (Vesper5/Ritual of the Moon).
